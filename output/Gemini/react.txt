**A History of Coyote Hills Regional Park**

Coyote Hills Regional Park, nestled on the southeastern shore of the San Francisco Bay in Fremont, California, boasts a rich history spanning thousands of years, transitioning from an ancient indigenous homeland to a vital natural preserve and recreational area.

Long before European arrival, the Coyote Hills area was home to the ancestors of the Ohlone people. For millennia, indigenous communities, particularly the Tuibun band, thrived in the fertile riparian marshes, utilizing the abundant resources for hunting and gathering. Archaeological evidence, including 2,000-year-old shellmound sites, attests to their enduring presence and sophisticated interaction with the landscape. The term "Ohlone" encompasses a loose group of Native American tribes in Northern California sharing common linguistic and cultural ties, and the Tuibun were significant inhabitants of this specific locale.

In more recent centuries, the natural salt marshes of the region became valuable for salt production. Over time, salt harvesting operations were consolidated, with Cargill becoming a major player. While extensive areas were dedicated to salt production, many of these marshlands have since been sold back to conservation agencies around the Bay, contributing to the preservation efforts that define the park today.

A pivotal moment in Coyote Hills' modern history occurred in the mid-1960s. Following the departure of a previous "project" (likely related to industrial or salt production activities), the area was redesignated for public use. In 1967, Coyote Hills officially joined the East Bay Regional Park District, marking its transformation into a public park dedicated to conservation and recreation.

Since its dedication, Coyote Hills Regional Park has become a cherished natural asset. Spanning nearly 978 acres, it preserves critical wetland habitats and the distinctive small range of hills that afford panoramic views of the San Francisco Bay, several trans-bay bridges, and the surrounding cities and mountain ranges. The park serves as an important education center, an outdoor recreation site, and a vital wildlife habitat, continuing to honor both its natural heritage and its deep historical roots.
