Coyote Hills Regional Park, a distinctive landform rising from the flatlands along the eastern shore of San Francisco Bay, boasts a rich and complex history spanning millions of years of geological activity, thousands of years of human habitation, and several centuries of land-use changes. Located in Fremont, California, this 975-acre park is a cherished natural and cultural preserve within the East Bay Regional Park District (EBRPD).

**Geological Origins:**
The Coyote Hills themselves are not volcanic, but rather an uplifted block of bedrock, primarily composed of sandstone and shale, formed over millions of years through tectonic forces. They are part of the East Bay Hills, pushed up along the Hayward Fault system. The low-lying hills, reaching a maximum elevation of about 250 feet, provide a dramatic contrast to the surrounding baylands and marshes, creating diverse microclimates and habitats.

**Indigenous Heritage: The Ohlone People:**
For at least 2,500 to 3,000 years, and potentially longer, the Coyote Hills area was a thriving home for the Ohlone people, specifically the ancestors of the contemporary Muwekma Ohlone Tribe. The hills offered a strategic location with abundant resources: freshwater springs, sheltered sites for villages, and access to the rich bounty of the bay and its marshes. The Ohlone hunted waterfowl, deer, and small game, gathered acorns and other plant foods, and harvested shellfish from the bay.

Evidence of their long-term presence is vividly seen in the numerous shellmounds (middens) found within the park. These mounds, some over 20 feet high and several hundred feet in diameter, are composed primarily of discarded shells from clams, oysters, and mussels, mixed with tools, animal bones, ash, and human burials. They represent centuries of continuous occupation and serve as invaluable archaeological sites, offering insights into the Ohlone's diet, technology, and spiritual practices. The largest and most prominent is known as the "Newark Shellmound," a significant cultural landmark.

**European Arrival and Ranching Era:**
With the arrival of Spanish explorers and missionaries in the late 18th century, the traditional Ohlone way of life was drastically disrupted. The lands surrounding Coyote Hills became part of vast Spanish land grants, eventually falling under Mexican rule. Following California's admission to the United States in 1850, the area was primarily used for cattle ranching, with various American families acquiring parcels of land. The hills provided grazing lands, while the bay marshlands were often diked for hay production.

**Industrialization and Development Threats (20th Century):**
The 20th century brought significant industrial and development pressures to the Coyote Hills. The most prominent industrial activity was salt production. Leslie Salt Company, a major player in the industry, acquired vast tracts of the baylands, including areas adjacent to the hills, for its elaborate system of evaporation ponds. These ponds, visible from the park today, transformed the natural salt marshes.

As the San Francisco Bay Area grew rapidly in the post-World War II era, the Coyote Hills' strategic location made them a target for various large-scale development proposals. These included plans for:
*   **An Oil Refinery:** Several proposals emerged in the 1960s to build an oil refinery, which would have irrevocably altered the landscape and ecology.
*   **A Nuclear Power Plant:** At one point, a nuclear power plant was considered for the site, raising significant environmental and safety concerns.
*   **Extensive Housing Developments:** Major housing projects were frequently proposed, threatening to cover the hillsides and destroy the remaining natural habitats and archaeological sites.

**Conservation and Park Establishment:**
The growing recognition of the unique ecological value of the baylands and the cultural significance of the Ohlone shellmounds, coupled with strong public opposition to industrial and residential development, fueled a powerful conservation movement. Local citizens, environmental groups, and the East Bay Regional Park District (EBRPD) began advocating for the preservation of Coyote Hills.

The EBRPD, founded in 1934, had a long-standing vision for creating a regional park system. In the late 1960s and early 1970s, the District, along with federal and state partners, embarked on a multi-phase land acquisition program. Through a combination of purchases from private landowners (including Leslie Salt, which sold off some of its less productive salt pond areas and uplands), and significant public funding, the core lands of Coyote Hills were secured.

Coyote Hills Regional Park officially opened to the public in 1967, though land acquisition and development continued for years. A key focus was on preserving the fragile archaeological sites while making the park accessible for recreation and education.

**Coyote Hills Today:**
Today, Coyote Hills Regional Park is a vibrant blend of natural beauty, cultural heritage, and recreational opportunities. It offers miles of trails for hiking, biking, and wildlife viewing, providing panoramic vistas of the San Francisco Bay, the Dumbarton Bridge, and the surrounding East Bay hills. The park is a critical habitat for a wide array of wildlife, including migratory birds, raptors, and various small mammals. Restoration efforts have focused on enhancing native plant communities and improving marsh habitats.

The Visitor Center provides educational exhibits about the park's natural and cultural history, emphasizing the legacy of the Ohlone people and the unique ecology of the baylands. Coyote Hills stands as a testament to successful conservation, preserving a vital piece of the region's past and present for future generations to explore and appreciate.
