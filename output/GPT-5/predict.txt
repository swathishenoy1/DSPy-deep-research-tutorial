Coyote Hills is one of the East Bay’s emblematic shoreline parks, where low, tawny hills meet the marshes and sloughs of southern San Francisco Bay. Its history is layered: a deep Indigenous presence shaped by bayland abundance; mission, rancho, and American agricultural eras; 20th‑century industrial and military uses along the shoreline; and, since the late 1960s, evolving public stewardship as part of the East Bay Regional Park District (EBRPD).

Indigenous homelands and the bay’s edge
- For thousands of years before European contact, the bayshore around today’s Coyote Hills sustained Ohlone peoples, including Chochenyo-speaking communities such as the Tuibun. Villages, seasonal camps, and shellmounds ringed the margins of the hills and tidal marshes, where people harvested shellfish, fish, waterfowl, and tule reeds, and gathered acorns and seeds from the nearby uplands.
- Archaeological sites within the park document this long continuity of use, while present-day Ohlone descendants maintain cultural ties. EBRPD’s Coyote Hills Visitor Center interprets this history, and the park’s reconstructed village area is used for school programs and public events led in collaboration with Ohlone cultural practitioners.

Colonial, rancho, and American eras
- The founding of Mission San José in 1797 set off profound disruptions. Many local Ohlone people were drawn into the mission system; disease and coercive labor devastated communities.
- After secularization in the 1830s–40s, the area became part of the Mexican rancho landscape dominated by cattle grazing. Following U.S. annexation, American farmers established dairies and crop fields on the alluvial flats east of the hills. The nearby Patterson family’s ranch—now Ardenwood Historic Farm—was one of the region’s major 19th‑century operations and influenced land use in the Coyote Hills vicinity.

Industry, infrastructure, and altered shorelines
- The 20th century transformed the South Bay shoreline. Companies built levees to create evaporation ponds for commercial salt production, reengineering former tidal marshes. Remnants of pond levees and borrow ditches still shape the hydrology west of the hills.
- Regional infrastructure further altered the setting. The first Dumbarton Bridge (1927) created a direct bay crossing just south of the park; a modern replacement opened in 1982. Alameda Creek was straightened and channelized for flood control in the mid‑20th century, changing sediment and freshwater flows to nearby marshes.
- During World War II and the Cold War, hilltops in the Coyote Hills area hosted communications and related military facilities. Most structures were later removed, but foundations and graded pads remain as traces of that period.

Creating a regional park (1967) and early interpretation
- Amid postwar industrial expansion, local conservationists and the EBRPD moved to protect remaining open space along the East Bay shoreline. Coyote Hills Regional Park opened to the public in 1967, securing both the low hills and adjacent marshlands as a landscape-scale unit.
- From its early years, the park emphasized natural history and Ohlone cultural interpretation. The Bay View Trail loop and spur routes across the hills provided panoramic access; boardwalks and levee-top paths led visitors into cattail- and tule-lined wetlands alive with shorebirds and raptors.

A connected conservation landscape
- In the 1970s, the federal government established what is now the Don Edwards San Francisco Bay National Wildlife Refuge on lands surrounding Coyote Hills. The park and refuge together created one of the Bay Area’s largest continuous protected bayland complexes, linked by the San Francisco Bay Trail and the Alameda Creek Trail.
- Large-scale wetland restoration—especially the South Bay Salt Pond Restoration Project launched in the 2000s—has incrementally reintroduced tidal action to former ponds near the Dumbarton corridor, improving habitat for fish, waterfowl, and endangered species such as the Ridgway’s rail and salt marsh harvest mouse.

Expansion, restoration, and contemporary stewardship
- In the 2010s, EBRPD acquired portions of the historic Patterson Ranch lands adjoining the park, expanding Coyote Hills eastward. The district has been converting former pastures to a mosaic of seasonal wetlands, alkali meadow, and upland grassland, while protecting archaeological sites and creating new public access points and trails.
- The park’s interpretive staff and Ohlone partners have continued and expanded cultural programming, including village-life demonstrations and annual gatherings that highlight language, craft, and ecological knowledge.
- Today, Coyote Hills is also a focal point for regional planning and environmental debates: adapting shoreline parks to sea-level rise, coordinating recreation with habitat protection, and addressing proposed urban development on nearby low-lying Newark baylands.

What Coyote Hills represents
- Coyote Hills’ history is a compressed story of the South Bay: deep-time Indigenous stewardship; a colonial rupture and the rancho era; the straight lines of levees, bridges, and channels overlaying a sinuous tidal world; and, more recently, deliberate efforts to reckon with that legacy through conservation, cultural collaboration, and restoration.
- As a gateway landscape, the park offers living evidence that the East Bay shoreline is both a cultural homeland and an ecological engine—and that its future depends on honoring both.

Key milestones (select)
- Prehistory to 18th century: Ohlone (Chochenyo-speaking) peoples, including the Tuibun, inhabit the hills–marsh ecotone; shellmounds and village sites accumulate over millennia.
- 1797–1830s: Mission San José era; demographic collapse and displacement of local communities.
- 1840s–early 1900s: Rancho grazing, then American dairying and farming on adjacent flats; shoreline gradually modified.
- Early–mid 1900s: Leveed salt ponds, military communications uses on hilltops; Alameda Creek channelized; Dumbarton Bridge corridor developed.
- 1967: Coyote Hills Regional Park opens under EBRPD.
- 1970s–present: Don Edwards NWR established adjacent; long-term habitat protection and trail connectivity.
- 2000s–present: South Bay Salt Pond Restoration underway; EBRPD acquires Patterson Ranch acreage; new restoration and public access projects at the park.
- Ongoing: Collaboration with Ohlone descendants for cultural stewardship; planning for climate resilience and balanced public use.
