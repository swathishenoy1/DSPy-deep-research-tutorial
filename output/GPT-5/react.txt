Coyote Hills Regional Park: A Concise History

Ancestral homelands and the Tuibun Ohlone
For thousands of years before European contact, the low, grass-covered hills and tidal marshes along the southeastern San Francisco Bay were home to Ohlone-speaking peoples. In the Coyote Hills area, the Tuibun Ohlone established villages and seasonal camps, drawing on the estuary’s rich fisheries, waterfowl, and plant resources, with acorns as a dietary staple. Shellmounds—cultural deposits built up over generations—mark this long presence; at Coyote Hills, archaeological sites include Tuibun Ohlone shellmounds dating back roughly two millennia, preserved today within the park.

Colonial, mission, and ranch eras
Spanish colonization in the late 18th century and the ensuing mission period reshaped Indigenous life throughout the East Bay. After secularization and the American conquest, the Coyote Hills landscape entered a new phase of ranching and agriculture in the 19th century. Dairies operated in and around the hills (a legacy remembered in the park’s Dairy Glen group camp name), and farming, grazing, and quarrying activities became part of the working landscape.

Wetland conversion, salt production, and duck clubs
As the Bay Area urbanized in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, large portions of the South Bay’s tidal marshes were diked and converted to salt ponds, and private duck hunting clubs appeared along the bay margins. The Coyote Hills area reflected these broader shoreline trends: portions of the adjacent wetlands were managed for salt and waterfowl, while quarrying and other extractive uses continued nearby. These activities altered tidal flows and habitats but also, in a paradoxical way, kept significant tracts from residential development, leaving open space that would later be conserved.

Cold War and research uses
During the early Cold War, the federal government constructed a Project NIKE anti-aircraft missile installation in the Coyote Hills. After the NIKE site was decommissioned, Stanford Research Institute (now SRI International) used the former military facilities and adjacent marshlands for biosonar research. By the mid-1960s, these uses wound down, creating an opening for public acquisition and conservation.

Becoming a regional park (1967) and early interpretive focus
Coyote Hills Regional Park was dedicated to public use in 1967 under the East Bay Regional Park District (EBRPD). From the outset, park staff emphasized both environmental education and cultural interpretation, centering the Ohlone presence and the ecological story of the South Bay marshes and uplands. The Visitor Center developed exhibits on Ohlone life, including a tule-reed boat constructed using traditional methods, and naturalist-led programs introduced generations of visitors to the park’s birds, butterflies, and marsh and grassland ecology.

Conservation, trails, and a regional refuge mosaic
Over subsequent decades, EBRPD expanded trails and naturalist programming while protecting sensitive cultural sites. The park’s hills and wetlands became a valued vantage on the broader San Francisco Bay estuary. The shoreline and marshes adjacent to Coyote Hills interconnect with the Don Edwards San Francisco Bay National Wildlife Refuge, giving the park a key role in a larger network of conserved habitats. The Alameda Creek Trail along the park’s north side and the Bayview Trail within the park opened up new public access while concentrating use to protect wildlife and archaeology.

Patterson Ranch: a long campaign to secure and restore the eastern gateway
For more than 25 years, community advocates and EBRPD leadership engaged the Patterson family and local jurisdictions to prevent intensive suburban development on the historic Patterson Ranch lands east of the park. In 2014, through the Regional Parks Foundation, EBRPD received its largest-ever land donation: a $14+ million gift of Patterson Ranch property that expanded Coyote Hills by approximately 25 percent and secured the park’s eastern gateway. The district undertook extensive public outreach and environmental review, culminating in Board certification of the Final Environmental Impact Report and approval of a Land Use Plan Amendment in September 2019.

Restoration and public access (2019–2025)
Guided by that plan, EBRPD launched a major restoration and public access project on the former ranchlands. The work restored approximately 170 acres of oak savanna, seasonal wetlands, and riparian corridors, while adding new parking, picnic areas, restrooms, accessible trails, and wildlife overlooks. These improvements opened in Fall 2025, connecting more visitors to the park’s cultural and natural history and enhancing ecological function along the bay margin.

Centering Indigenous language and place names (2022–present)
In partnership with the Muwekma Ohlone Tribe, EBRPD updated 35 trail markers in 2022 to include names in Chochenyo, the local Ohlone language. The initiative restores original place names to the landscape and deepens interpretation at Coyote Hills—known in Chochenyo as Máyyan Šaatošikma. Annual events such as the Gathering of Ohlone Peoples at the Visitor Center further honor living Ohlone cultures and their enduring relationships with the land.

Today
Today, Coyote Hills Regional Park—encompassing roughly 1,266 acres of rolling hills and preserved wetlands—stands as both a cultural landscape and a biodiversity refuge. Its history layers millennia of Ohlone lifeways, 19th- and 20th-century ranching and industry, the Cold War’s imprint, and, most recently, community-driven conservation and cultural revitalization. The park’s trails, programs, and restored habitats offer a living classroom where the past informs a more inclusive and resilient future for the San Francisco Bay shoreline.
