Coyote Hills Regional Park: A History

Introduction
Coyote Hills Regional Park, dedicated in 1967 and managed by the East Bay Regional Park District (EBRPD), lies on the southeastern shore of San Francisco Bay in present-day Fremont, California. Its 1,200+ acres encompass grass-covered hills and remnant marshes that sit amid a landscape profoundly shaped by Indigenous lifeways, Spanish missionization and Mexican ranching, industrial salt production, military infrastructure, and late‑20th‑ and 21st‑century conservation and restoration (EBRPD, Coyote Hills page).

Ancestral homelands: the Tuibun Ohlone and the marshlands
Long before Euro-American settlement, the low hills, freshwater marshes, and sloughs around today’s Coyote Hills supported the Tuibun, one of the Ohlone (Chochenyo-speaking) tribal communities. The wetlands’ seasonal abundance—tule, waterfowl, fish, shellfish, and terrestrial game—anchored a complex hunter‑gatherer economy that stored staples, sustained relatively dense populations, and supported intricate ceremonial and social life. Archaeological deposits in the park include 2,000‑year‑old Tuibun Ohlone shellmound (mounded) sites, which preserve mortuary and ritual materials and have been a focus of research for over a century (EBRPD, Coyote Hills page).

Archaeology at Coyote Hills and adjacent mounds
Within the modern park boundary, CA‑ALA‑328 (the Patterson Mound) is a major mortuary shellmound located roughly a mile east of the hills proper. Recorded in 1909 and excavated intermittently from the 1930s to late 1960s by Berkeley, San Francisco State, and CSU East Bay teams, the mound has yielded extensive bone, groundstone, charmstones, worked antler, and shell bead assemblages (Bickel 1976; CSU East Bay ALA‑328 page). ALA‑328 is predominantly Middle Period in age and shows primarily flexed interments, with rich variability in associated artifacts and evidence for feasting and ritual consistent with broader San Francisco Bay shellmound interpretations (Davis & Treganza 1959; Bickel 1976).

Just beyond the park to the east-southeast lies CA‑ALA‑329 (the Ryan Mound), long used from roughly 200 BCE into the Mission era. Extensive excavations by Stanford and San José State University during the 1950s–60s documented hundreds of burials and associated goods; later analyses have explored social differentiation, mortuary patterning (notably a high incidence of flexed and some seated burials), and paleoepidemiology (Leventhal 1993; Jurmain et al. 2009; Norman 2023). Across the Coyote Hills complex, four principal mounds (including ALA‑12 and ALA‑13) articulate a long, continuous Indigenous presence and ritual landscape, which contemporary Muwekma Ohlone perspectives have emphasized as cemeteries and ceremonial places rather than mere “middens.”

Spanish missionization, Mexican ranching, and 19th‑century change
From 1797, Mission San José drew in and constrained Ohlone communities through conversion, labor, and resettlement; the 1830s secularization dispersed mission lands as ranchos, accelerating demographic and cultural disruptions for the Tuibun and neighboring groups. In the 19th century the bay margin saw dairying, small farms, duck-hunting clubs, rock quarrying, and intensified resource extraction. EBRPD summarizes these overlapping land uses—mission/settler ranching, a dairy, hunting, and quarrying—as part of the Coyote Hills landscape’s complex palimpsest (EBRPD, Coyote Hills page).

Industrial salt and the transformation of the South Bay
Industrial-scale solar saltworks radically reengineered South Bay marshes into a mosaic of diked evaporation ponds beginning in the mid‑19th century. Commercial production started in 1854 near Mount Eden; by the early 20th century, dozens of operators had consolidated into Leslie Salt, and by the 1930s nearly half of the South Bay’s historical tidal marsh had been converted to ponds. At peak, roughly 36,000 acres of salt ponds existed around the Bay. In 1978 Cargill purchased Leslie Salt, inheriting extensive holdings and mineral rights; the system included processing and bittern-storage facilities in Newark, immediately south and west of Coyote Hills (South Bay Salt Pond Restoration Project, Background Report).

This industrial landscape persists in altered form today as restoration partners convert selected ponds back to tidal marsh while retaining others as managed pond habitat. The adjacency of Coyote Hills to the Don Edwards San Francisco Bay National Wildlife Refuge (established in the late 1970s with initial salt pond acquisitions) knits the park into one of the West Coast’s signature wetland restoration efforts, balancing avian-habitat values of shallow ponds with the re-creation of tidal marsh processes and resilience to sea-level rise (EBRPD, Coyote Hills page; South Bay Salt Pond Restoration Project).

Cold War militarization and research
Elements of mid‑20th‑century national defense and science also touched Coyote Hills. EBRPD notes a former NIKE missile site on the hills and a biosonar research facility among the land uses preceding park dedication. These facilities reflect the Bay Area’s Cold War air-defense network and a period of intensive coastal and estuarine research and testing (EBRPD, Coyote Hills page).

Creating a regional park (1967) and evolving interpretation
Coyote Hills Regional Park was dedicated to public use in 1967, preserving a blend of marsh, grassland, and ridge-top vistas with connections to the Alameda Creek Trail and the Don Edwards refuge (EBRPD, Coyote Hills page). The park’s visitor center developed exhibits emphasizing Ohlone material culture (e.g., tule-reed technologies) and Bay ecology. In collaboration with the Muwekma Ohlone Tribe, EBRPD has advanced interpretive shifts that foreground Indigenous sovereignty, language, and place. In 2022 the District unveiled updated signage for 35 trails that includes names in Chochenyo, the language of the park’s First Peoples; the park itself is identified as Máyyan Šaatošikma in Chochenyo (EBRPD, Coyote Hills page). Annual gatherings and programing with Ohlone leaders further integrate living cultural perspectives with archaeological stewardship.

Edges, infrastructure, and the Dumbarton corridor
Coyote Hills sits at the eastern anchor of the Dumbarton corridor, with the Dumbarton Bridge and associated levees, trails, and former industrial sites shaping access and hydrology. The nearby Dumbarton Quarry—long an aggregate source—has been repurposed as an EBRPD campground and staging area, providing overflow parking during peak Coyote Hills visitation and symbolizing the broader regional arc from extractive to recreational and conservation land uses (EBRPD, Coyote Hills page: parking advisory referencing Dumbarton Quarry Campground).

Expansion, restoration, and adjacent land-use controversies
- Patterson Ranch and park expansion: EBRPD’s materials document donations and acquisitions associated with the Patterson lands, which expanded the park’s lowland footprint and enabled a Coyote Hills Restoration and Public Access Project focused on wetland enhancement and new trails. These transactions illustrate the patchwork of public, private, and mitigation-driven conservation along the urban bay edge (EBRPD, Coyote Hills page: Patterson Donation Maps; Restoration and Public Access Project).
- Newark bayfront (Areas 3 & 4): Immediately south of the park and refuge, longstanding proposals to develop former salt pond or low-lying bayfront lands (often referenced as “Areas 3 & 4”) have generated sustained regional debate about wetland restoration, sea-level rise risk, and habitat connectivity. While outside the park, outcomes there influence ecological and public-access continuity along the South Bay shoreline.
- Archaeological stewardship and repatriation: Decades of early 20th‑century excavation removed hundreds of burials from Coyote Hills mounds. Subsequent collaborations with Ohlone communities, including repatriations of remains in the 1990s, and evolving on‑site protection of mound areas reflect wider shifts in California archaeology toward tribal sovereignty, ethical curation, and restricted access to sensitive sites (Norman 2023; Leventhal 1993, as summarized in Norman 2023).

Coyote Hills today
Today Coyote Hills functions as a cultural landscape as much as a park: a place where Chochenyo place-names, Ohlone archaeology, and ongoing tribal presence meet the restored marshes, levees, and pondscapes of the South Bay. Its trails connect to the Alameda Creek corridor and Don Edwards NWR; its ridges overlook the industrial-to-ecological transformation of the South Bay saltworks; and its interpretive programs increasingly proceed in partnership with Muwekma Ohlone leaders. As climate adaptation and bayland restoration advance, Coyote Hills stands at the nexus of cultural stewardship and estuarine resilience.
