Coyote Hills: A Layered History of Estuary, Industry, and Parkland

Introduction: Landscape and Names
Coyote Hills rises as a cluster of low, wind-scrubbed knolls on the eastern shore of San Francisco Bay, west of Fremont along the lower Alameda Creek–Mowry Slough complex. Its Spanish/Mexican-era name survives on an 1860 U.S. survey plat as Potrero de los Cerritos—“pasture of the little hills”—capturing both the landform and its pastoral use after mission secularization (Calisphere, “Plat of the Rancho Potrero de los Cerritos,” 1860). Today the site anchors Coyote Hills Regional Park in the East Bay Regional Park District (EBRPD), framed by restored and managed wetlands, surviving salt ponds, and the Dumbarton corridor. The park’s history interleaves deep Indigenous tenure, Spanish and Mexican colonial projects, American-era industrial transformation, Cold War militarization, and contemporary conservation and controversy.

Indigenous Tenure and Estuary Lifeways
Long before European contact, the lower Alameda Creek–Mowry Slough estuary supported dense, seasonally patterned lifeways among Ohlone-speaking communities. Park interpretation and regional scholarship commonly identify the Coyote Hills area with the Tuibun, a local Chochenyo Ohlone community. Estuaries like those fringing the hills furnished acorns from nearby oak savannas, tule for housing and canoes, fish runs, waterfowl, and shellfish, producing both dietary staples and durable shellmounds—cultural and stratigraphic records of habitation and ceremony. Coyote Hills preserves one of the Bay’s few remaining shellmounds; most of the Bay Area’s 400+ were destroyed by urbanization and extractive archaeology in the 19th and 20th centuries (Clio, Coyote Hills entry). Ohlone salt-harvesting from natural brine pools in the South Bay predated commercial works and was later adapted by Spanish missionaries—an example of Indigenous ecological knowledge shaping colonial practices (South Bay Salt Pond Restoration Project, 2002 background report).

Spanish Missionization and Mexican Rancho Transformations (1797–1840s)
The establishment of Mission San José in 1797 in Ohlone territory marked a profound rupture. As nearby missions at San Francisco and Santa Clara competed for labor, Mission San José drew in Ohlone and, increasingly, peoples from farther afield—Miwok, Yokuts, Patwin, Wappo, and Nisenan—through coercive expeditions, enforced resettlement, and an assimilation regime that prohibited Indigenous languages and ceremonies (Cal Indian Missions, Mission San José). The mission economy introduced large herds of cattle, goats, and pigs that overgrazed and trampled Bay margins, undermining Indigenous estuarine resource bases. Epidemics (measles, influenza, smallpox) and mission conditions precipitated severe demographic collapse. Resistance was frequent; punitive expeditions targeted Ohlone-speaking groups (e.g., an 1805 expedition against the Luecha), and multi-tribal campaigns like Estanislao’s 1828 actions challenged mission authority (Cal Indian Missions).

Mexico’s 1834 secularization aimed to redistribute mission assets—nominally half to Indigenous communities—but, in practice, few Indian pueblos were established and control shifted to private ranching. In the Coyote Hills reach, the Potrero de los Cerritos grant was confirmed in U.S. courts after annexation, with the 1860 plat showing boundaries, marshes, and the “little hills” that gave the rancho its name and purpose (Calisphere, Rancho Potrero de los Cerritos). This transition converted mission herds into privatized pastoral economies; Indigenous people, drastically reduced in number, continued as laborers on ranchos while losing secure access to ancestral estuaries.

American Annexation, Rails, and Salt: Engineering the Estuary (1850s–1970s)
Commercial salt extraction on the Bay began in the 1850s and accelerated through the late 19th and 20th centuries as dozens of companies diked and leveed tidal wetlands into an integrated evaporative system. By the 1930s, nearly half of the South Bay’s historical tidal marshes had been converted to ponds; at their peak, salt ponds covered roughly 36,000 acres Bay-wide (South Bay Salt Pond Restoration Project, 2002). Industry consolidated under Leslie Salt during the Depression and mid-century, later acquired by Cargill in 1978. Around Newark and Mowry Slough—immediately adjacent to Coyote Hills—intake ponds, sequential evaporators, high-salinity “pickle” ponds, and crystallizers formed a sprawling engineered landscape connected by levees, channels, and seasonal harvest railways. Newark hosted processing and bittern-storage facilities, with extremely saline by-products presenting persistent management and regulatory challenges. While the pondscape provided surrogate habitats for certain birds (e.g., snowy plover) and nearby harbor seals—Mowry Slough holds the Bay’s largest pupping site—it simultaneously fragmented and eliminated vast extents of tidal marsh, altered hydrology, and created predator corridors along levees. Invasive smooth cordgrass (Spartina alterniflora) and non-native predators (e.g., red fox) added further ecological stressors (South Bay Salt Pond Restoration Project, 2002).

Transportation infrastructure reinforced these changes. The Southern Pacific’s Dumbarton Rail Bridge—completed circa 1910 as the first structure to span San Francisco Bay—linked Newark to the Peninsula via a ten-mile cutoff. Built with marine engineering feats (spans floated in by tug, dredging by stern-wheelers) and over 100,000 tons of rock, it carried freight and commuters until the last freight run in 1982, underscoring Newark’s industrial connectivity during the salt era (City of Newark History, Dumbarton Bridge). The bridge and associated rail spurs facilitated movement of bulk commodities to market, integrating the Newark–Coyote Hills shoreline into regional production networks.

From Cold War Battery to Regional Park (1956–1967)
The core of today’s Coyote Hills Regional Park originated not from a salt-company transfer but from military demobilization. San Francisco Defense Site SF-37, a Nike-Ajax surface-to-air missile installation established in 1956, was decommissioned in March 1963. The administrative barracks were later adapted as the park’s visitor center; the launcher area—with twelve launchers and magazines for thirty missiles—was bulldozed and filled; the control area two miles away remained in civilian use (Clio, Coyote Hills). Between Army and park tenures, Stanford Research Institute briefly operated a mid-1960s sonar project on marine mammals. In 1967, the site joined the East Bay Regional Park District, inaugurating a long-standing interpretive emphasis on Ohlone history, estuarine ecology, and the ethics of archaeology. The park’s preserved shellmound and reconstructed Ohlone village structures (tule house, sweat lodge, shade shelter, pit house) have served as focal points for education—set amid broader shifts toward consultation with Ohlone representatives and compliance with the Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act (NAGPRA) in caring for ancestral remains and cultural items. Adjacent marshlands long held by salt interests increasingly passed to conservation ownership from the 1970s onward (e.g., U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service acquisitions), situating Coyote Hills within a mosaic of protected baylands (Clio; South Bay Salt Pond Restoration Project, 2002).

Restoration, Expansion, and Controversy in the 21st Century
Coyote Hills now sits at the heart of two entwined regional projects: the South Bay Salt Pond Restoration—an unprecedented effort to convert parts of the pondscape back to tidal marsh and seasonal wetlands while retaining some managed ponds for pond-adapted species—and EBRPD’s expansion and restoration of park uplands and marsh margins. Restoration carries unavoidable tradeoffs: desalinating ponds and breaching levees can recover marsh processes and water quality but may reduce habitat for species specialized to hypersaline ponds; new channels and marsh edges can also alter predator dynamics and vegetation (e.g., Spartina hybridization risks) (South Bay Salt Pond Restoration Project, 2002).

Locally, the most visible controversy concerned development pressure at Patterson Ranch, an expansive tract east of the original park. For over 25 years, the Patterson family pursued residential proposals while grassroots coalitions—Friends of Coyote Hills and the Citizens’ Committee to Complete the Refuge—opposed urbanization, advocating for park expansion and bayland connectivity. In 2014, after long negotiations led by EBRPD General Manager Robert E. Doyle, the Park District received a major land donation valued at over $14 million, expanding Coyote Hills by about 25%—the largest such gift in District history via the Regional Parks Foundation (EBRPD, Coyote Hills Restoration and Public Access Project). Following extensive public outreach and environmental review, the EBRPD Board in 2019 certified the Final EIR, adopted CEQA findings (including a Statement of Overriding Considerations) and a Mitigation Monitoring and Reporting Program, and approved a Land Use Plan Amendment. The project restores roughly 170 acres of oak savanna, seasonal wetlands, and riparian habitat and adds public access amenities; new facilities opened in fall 2025 (EBRPD).

These actions intersect with additional contemporary concerns:
- Tribal consultation and representation: Planning and interpretation increasingly involve Ohlone representatives (including the Muwekma Ohlone Tribe, whose members assert ancestral ties to the Mission San José/Coyote Hills area), reflecting evolving standards for cultural stewardship and interpretation. Processes emphasize appropriate treatment of ancestral sites while navigating legal constraints and agency mandates.
- Sea-level rise and marsh migration: The park’s low-lying bay margins and pond levees face accelerating risks from rising seas and storm surges. Managers weigh where to breach levees, how to create space for landward marsh migration, and how to align upland restorations (e.g., oak savannas) to buffer future ecotone shifts, all within regulatory constraints on brine discharge and water-quality standards (South Bay Salt Pond Restoration Project, 2002; EBRPD 2019 approvals).
- Legacy industrial hazards: Managing and, where appropriate, desalinating bittern and residual brines near Newark remains complex, costly, and tightly regulated; decisions about which ponds to retain in production involve ongoing coordination with Cargill and the Don Edwards San Francisco Bay National Wildlife Refuge (South Bay Salt Pond Restoration Project, 2002).

Conclusion: Continuity and Change on the Edge of the Bay
Coyote Hills encapsulates the Bay margin’s long arc: Indigenous stewardship of estuary and oak savanna; colonial disruptions and privatization under mission and rancho regimes; industrial re-engineering that converted marsh to ponds and corridors; and a late-20th- to 21st-century turn toward protection, restoration, and more inclusive historical interpretation. The park’s shellmound and reconstructed village remind visitors that the estuary’s productivity—and the knowledge systems that sustained it—precede the levees, bridges, and batteries that later defined the horizon. Today, as restoration proceeds under the twin pressures of development and climate change, Coyote Hills’ history cautions that managing the edge of the Bay is as much about honoring deep relationships to place as it is about moving rock, water, and earth.
